An outsider's look at the legal sector

I recently had to prepare a small claims tribunal case and the experience has left me with a huge cultural shock. Coming from the tech sector, where information is freely available, legal information is protected by gatekeepers known as lawyers and it is almost impossible to traverse the abyss without the assistance of one.

It is extremely difficult to find a single piece of information. To illustrate, I would like to find out if I can claim $16,000 under the small claims tribunal with only single party consent. The state court's website states that the maximum amount I can claim is $10,000. I had to trawl through the Small Claims Tribunal Act to find out that I am allowed, under section 9 of the act, to abandon part of the claim to give the tribunal jurisdiction over the case. That is not all. Under section 2 of the The Small Claims Tribunal Amendment Act which was passed by Parliament on 9 July 2018 and assented to by the President on 2 August 2018, the prescribed limit is increased from $10,000 to $20,000 or any other sum the Minister may, after consultation with the Chief Justice, prescribe by order in the Gazette. So, why does the state court's website still state $10,000 as the limit? Was there something in the Gazette? The eGazette is available at an annual subscription of $2,597.08. That piece of information is locked behind a paywall.

The eGazette is not the only thing you have to pay for. Want to get a company business profile? That will be $5.50. Want to check if a person is bankrupt? That will be $6. Certified translation? I have a whole separate article on it. I swear, the legal sector pioneered micro-transactions, not the gaming sector.

The printing press was invented in 1468. The internet was commoditized in the 1990s. However, the legal sector didn't seem to get the memo. The primary means of disseminating legal information today is through 1 on 1 sessions known as legal clinics. Unless you are really destitute, you only get a 20 min free trial with the CJC. Isn't there a better way of disseminating information? I understand there may be obligations to posting legal advice on a blog, but cant you prefix it with a disclaimer stating that it is for information only? There are many sharp, logical individuals out there who will benefit from access to the information and advice. They can determine if the information is relevant to their case and analyse if it is worth the risk to bank on that piece of information or to seek professional legal advice. It is high time these luddites think about scaling up. How many people can you help at a pro bono legal clinic? For reference, my blog currently sees approximately 50 unique visitors daily. Even if only 10% benefit from my content, I help at least 5 people daily. As readership increases, it scales up without me needing to spend more of my own time.

A quick google search reveals 2 websites out there providing legal information for free. However, both seem to be affiliated to certain law firms. The articles provide you with just enough information and implore you to contact their lawyers for more information. Why aren't lawyers forthcoming with sharing information solely out of goodwill? Let's look at the state court's website. After all, they are the definitive authority, have no profit motive, and claim that any member of the public should be able to file small claims without the assistance of a lawyer. You could become a competent programmer by relying solely on free online materials. The barrier to entry to the profession is a sharp analytical mind and the grit to keep working at getting better. If the same is true for the legal sector, then there should be no qualms about making information freely available online.

While preparing for my small claims tribunal case, I have spent a lot of time painstakingly researching various topics. Over the next few articles, I will share the information freely. I am not a lawyer, I can't guarantee the accuracy of the information, but I believe in sharing the knowledge. Semi accurate information is better than none.
